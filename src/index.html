Chief Technology Officer

ListGen Inc. | 7/2018 - 4/2019 Carmel Valley, CA

I joined ListGen Inc, a real estate software startup, right out of Stanford as the sole programmer, excited to learn how a tech company is built up from the very ground level. As the only full-time employee, I worked closely with the founder designing the business model and developing a small demo with HTML, CSS, and vanilla Javascript. Shortly before the demo was complete, I decided to submit a full redesign, and restarted the project from scratch with a brand-new vision for the software, which came with a new sense of ownership of the web application.


During the creation of a new demo with JQuery, we brought on two contracting programmers for the backend and an extra technical consultant. I assumed the title of CTO, along with the responsibilities of leading technical meetings, completing a full design of our NoSQL database, and keeping track of our business milestones. Using the completed demo of our core product, I presented our business model to an investor, successfully raising $75k in investment capital.


Having learned a huge amount in the process of creating the demo, I reflected on what I did well and how I could improve - thus, in order to create the world-class product that we were striving for, I decided to implement one more full rewrite of our software with the powerful framework, React. While I did not complete the application due to my leaving the company, the experience I had teaching myself new technologies was vital to my growth as a worker in the software industry. I parted ways with ListGen due to an itching for new opportunities. In my last month at the company, I trained a replacement for myself, and consolidated and documented the full codebase in order to leave the company as much on track as possible.

Associate Developer Intern

Capital Insurance Group | 6/2016-9/2016 Montery, CA

My summer internship at CIG was my first real job in software development, and I was eager to do my best after only having taken two Computer Sciences at Stanford. I landed this position after I committed to changing my major from Chemical Engineering to CS, and it was a chance for me to see how the technical department of a large company functioned. While I look back on my experience at CIG fondly, my biggest takeaway was what I do not want from a software development job. I met great people and learned a great deal on the projects I was assigned to, but it was a static environment where I was confined to my cubicle.


Along with bugfixes and maintentance, I worked on one major project during my three-month internship, which left a lasting positive effect on the company I wrote a script that cleansed all out-of-date, incorrect-input information in an Excel sheet that contained all claims records that must be sent to a partner company (and believe me, there was a lot of incorrect info). By standardizing the data, it allowed for much more efficient communication between CIG and their partner. Furthermore, I implemented extensive error checking in the system where claims are added to the sheet, forcing all inputs to be in the same format.


CIG was the first company that I worked with a team of other programmers under the Agile methodology. I learned the benefits of structured code delivery and collaboration, and working with a variety of programmers in my scrum team provided me with exposure to different specialities and methods of development. From learning to write efficient SQL queries to having my first taste of front-end web development, CIG was a vital stepping stone in allowing me to discover the world of software engineering.

Photolithography Technician

Stanford Nanofabrication Facility | 1/2015 - 1/2016 Stanford, CA

I came into Stanford aiming to be a Chemical Engineer, and figured that real laboratory experience as early as possible would be a great experience. I eventually found myself a starting member of the Stanford Undergraduate Monitoring Organization (SUMO) for the Stanford Nanofabrication Facility (SNF). My task was to work with a team of my peers to run systematic tests on the machines throughout the facility and take measurements in order to maintain that all machines were working correctly and document their operating health.


This position required strong teamwork, background knowledge of chemistry, and an ability to adapt quickly and learn the strict safety and cleanliness procedures of SNF. I worked in two specific areas of the lab: Photolithography and Dry Etching, each providing their own challenges and necessary theoretical knowledge to operate their respective machines correctly. As the growth of SUMO began to accelerate, I noticed a lack of accountability and slowdowns in the testing process. After identifying this, I worked to create a new standardized, less repetitive, list of monthly tasks to document all the required machines, and presented it to the coordinators of SUMO. Within a month, we had transitioned to this new system and were much more organized and efficient in our tasks. I continued to train new SUMO members using our new workflow and helped grow the organization significantly. I eventually left SUMO after I decided to switch to Computer Science, but my time in the Nanofabrication Facility was paramount in teaching me how to take the initiative in identifying problems and finding their solutions.

Martial Arts Instructor

Bodyworx Martial Arts | 2011-2015 Monterey, CA

If there is any one activity or experience that has defined my life the most, it is my introduction to martial arts when I was six years old. My parents signed me up for Tang Soo Do, a Korean style of karate, much to my fear. But I quickly grew to love it. It took eight years of training to consistently to begin teaching classes, and one year after that, I achieved my goal of a first degree black belt. It is a common saying in martial arts that achieving black belt is only the first step, where the real journey begins. This is 100% true. The amount of growth that I had, both technically and mentally, in Tang Soo Do in the two years between my first and second degree black belts was only exclipsed by my three years between my second and third. My third degree black belt was one of the most surreal moments of my life, as I finally caught up to my two instructors, who were both third degrees, as well.


My time teaching consistently was invaluable. I improved my confidence, my ability to speak to others, my ability to demonstrate different moves and their applications, and how to work with people of vastly different levels of experience and maturities. There was so much that went into planning full workouts and privately coaching; I had to center workouts around certain concepts and muscles, and tailor the workouts specifically to the students I was working with.When not on the mat teaching, I would be behind the front desk, ready to introduce new students to the martial arts world, and fostering the community of the school. My family at Bodyworx Martial Arts will always be with me, and I am outstandingly proud to represent a school who cares so much about its students.
